About

Software as both a technical and human problem.

My portfolio should not sound like every other junior developer portfolio. My Computer Science and Psychology background is part of how I think, build and communicate.

I approach software as both a technical and a human problem. I care about building systems that are reliable, understandable and genuinely useful to the people who rely on them.

My Computer Science background shapes how I think about structure, correctness, testing and maintainability. My Psychology background shapes how I think about users, confidence, accessibility and friction.

I’m especially interested in backend and full-stack systems where clear logic, good validation and thoughtful user flows work together. To me, good software is not only code that runs correctly. It is software that helps people make decisions, complete tasks and trust what the system is doing.

Why I build software

I am motivated by problems that sit at the intersection of technology and everyday life. ScoreRent began because I realised prospective tenants often lose money applying for rentals they realistically have little chance of securing. Rather than creating another CRUD application, I wanted to build something that could explain its reasoning and help people make better decisions.

SeaClear came from a similar place. Instead of simply displaying environmental data, the goal was to make complex water quality information understandable enough that someone could confidently decide whether it was safe to visit a beach.

How I think

I enjoy backend engineering because it combines problem solving with structure. I like breaking ambiguous problems into smaller components, designing clean interfaces between them and building systems that are easy to reason about.

Building under constraints

One lesson that has shaped me as an engineer is that constraints often force creativity. When my laptop stopped working, I still wanted to continue building software. Instead of stopping, I adapted my workflow by using browser-based development environments, cloud notebooks and online IDEs.

That experience changed how I think about development. It showed me that good engineering is not only about having ideal tools. It is about understanding the fundamentals well enough to keep moving when circumstances change. I have also fixed deployments and investigated issues from my phone while away from my desk, which reinforced the value of flexible workflows and resourcefulness.

Beyond code

Outside of software engineering, I have spent time tutoring university students and serving in student leadership roles. Teaching strengthened my ability to explain technical ideas clearly, while leadership taught me how to work with different people, coordinate projects and communicate under pressure.

Looking ahead

I am looking for opportunities where I can continue growing as a backend or full-stack software engineer. My long-term goal is to build software that combines strong engineering with thoughtful design: systems that are technically sound, transparent and genuinely useful to the people who depend on them.
